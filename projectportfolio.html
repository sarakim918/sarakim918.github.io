Spanish Is Fun

Spanish is Fun was a 6-week summer project focused on creating a mobile application for a local Spanish school. Developed for both iOS and Android platforms using Flutter, Xcode, and Android Studio, our team of three undergraduate students aimed to enhance the language learning experience through engaging exercises and interactive features. The app offered a variety of functionality, including flashcards, multiple-choice quizzes, and audio-visual aids for pronunciation practice. Our team also prioritized aesthetics and user experience, investing significant effort into design elements such as splash screens, logos, and visual content. The emphasis on quality permeated every aspect of the project, resulting in a polished and user-friendly application. The project's code remains under contract, so unfortunately there is no access.

Promoting Social Health for International Students

Promoting Social Health for International Students: A Human-Centered Design Approach to Support In-Situ Social Training, Reflection, and Connection was an on-campus research project I conducted with three other graduate students that focused on the unique challenges faced by international students. Leveraging social and collaborative computing values and human-centered design techniques, we aimed to understand and address the unique social health needs of this group. Our methodology included questionnaires, interviews, and user testing sessions, which allowed us to uncover problems, specify design implications, generate potential solutions, and develop a prototype application guided by our interviewees. The project details and findings are thoroughly documented in the paper to the left.

Hey Siri, Who's Listening?

Hey Siri, Who's Listening? was a solo research project I completed for a graduate seminar on Privacy and Security in Internet of Things (IoT) devices. This project focused on the Siri voice activation function found in most Apple devices. Through comprehensive at-home testing, I aimed to identify the frequency of accidental activations and examine the privacy implications of such behavior. The research revealed suspicious inconsistencies in Siri's behavior, particularly when comparing device responses in active mode versus sleep mode. These findings highlight potential privacy concerns related to unintended activations and data collection practices. The full project details, including methodology and results, are documented in the paper to the right.
